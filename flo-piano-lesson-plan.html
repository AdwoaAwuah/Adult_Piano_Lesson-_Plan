01
Foundation Phase

Hello, Piano — Posture, Notes & Your First Sounds

This week is about building confidence, understanding the instrument, and finding your seat at the keys without any pressure to perform.

🎯
Section 1
Learning Objectives
+
  • →Identify all white keys (C–B) by name and locate them on a full keyboard
  • →Achieve correct seated posture: bench height, wrist alignment, and curved fingers
  • →Understand the layout of the piano (treble/bass, octaves, landmark notes: Middle C, C4)
  • →Play individual notes with consistent, relaxed tone using proper finger weight
🤲
Section 2
Warm-Up & Finger Exercises
+
  • →Hand stretches (3 min): Wrist rotations, finger fans, and gentle knuckle presses. Never skip this — adult hands need warm-up time.
  • →"Raindrop" exercise (5 min): Press each white key from C to G with fingers 1–5, letting each note ring fully before moving. Focus: no tension in the wrist.
  • →Finger independence tap (3 min): Place hand flat on a table; lift each finger individually 10 times, especially fingers 4 and 5.
📖
Section 3
Music Theory
+
  • →The musical alphabet: A B C D E F G — introduce how it repeats across octaves using the black key groups (2s and 3s) as landmarks
  • →The staff: Treble clef, 5 lines, 4 spaces. Mnemonic: Every Good Boy Does Fine (lines); FACE (spaces)
  • →Note values intro: Whole note (4 beats), half note (2 beats), quarter note (1 beat). Clap examples while counting aloud
  • →Middle C: Location on the keyboard, on the staff (ledger line), and in both clefs
👂
Section 4
Ear Training & Improvisation
+
  • →High vs Low: Teacher plays two notes — student identifies which is higher. Repeat 8–10 rounds. Simple but builds foundational relative pitch.
  • →Sound & Sustain: Play Middle C and count how long it rings. Discuss how a piano produces sound (hammer striking string). Creates awareness of listening.
  • →Free exploration (5 min): Student plays any white keys they like, no rules. Improvisation from day one removes fear and encourages curiosity.
🎵
Section 5
Repertoire Suggestions
+
  • →Classical: "Ode to Joy" (Beethoven) — right hand only, C position, 5 notes. Iconic and immediately rewarding.
  • →Folk/Pop: "Mary Had a Little Lamb" — simple melodic pattern, good for finger numbering practice
  • →Adult-friendly goal: Choose 1 piece the student loves and listen to a recording together. Plant the seed of what they're working toward over 8 weeks.
📱
Section 6
Flowkey App Guided Practice
+
  • →Setup session: Configure the app — connect MIDI keyboard or use built-in touch keyboard. Set skill level to Beginner.
  • →Flowkey Exercise: "Note Recognition" — Use the app's note-name quiz to reinforce the musical alphabet. Aim for 90% accuracy on white keys by end of session.
  • →Flowkey Exercise: "Guided First Song" — Use the falling-note display to play "Ode to Joy" right hand. The visual feedback removes anxiety about reading music immediately.
  • →Home practice: 15 min/day on Flowkey. Goal: complete the "Week 1 Beginner Module" and earn the first in-app badge.
🟢 Flowkey App · Week 1 Module · ~15 min/day
💻 Online format: Share screen during lesson so teacher can observe Flowkey app usage in real time. Use video call's annotation feature to mark the keyboard diagram together.
🏠
Section 7
Offline Practice Activities
+
  • →Keyboard map: Print or draw a keyboard diagram. Label every white key. Put it on your fridge. Say the names aloud when you pass it.
  • →Silent finger taps: Tap finger sequences 1-2-3-4-5 and 5-4-3-2-1 on any flat surface — desk, knee, table — while watching TV or commuting.
  • →Music listening journal: Listen to one piece of piano music per day (suggest: Beethoven, Einaudi, or a jazz pianist). Write one word describing how it made you feel.
📊
Section 8
Assessment & Progress Tracking
+
  • →End-of-week check: Can the student name every white key when pointed to randomly? (Target: 100%)
  • →Performance marker: Play "Ode to Joy" RH with correct fingering and no pauses between notes
  • →Flowkey App metric: Screenshot the Week 1 module completion score. Share with teacher.
  • →Self-reflection (written): "What surprised me most about the piano this week?" One sentence is enough.
✨
Section 9
Motivation & Creative Challenge
+

🎯 Week 1 Creative Challenge

Using only the 5 white keys C D E F G, compose a 4-note melody that describes your mood today. Give it a title. There are no wrong answers — this is yours. Play it to the teacher (or record it) at the next lesson. This small act of creation is the beginning of your musical voice.

  • →Mindset reframe: Adults often feel embarrassed learning alongside children. Remember: adult brains understand music theory faster and have deeper emotional connection to music. Your maturity is an advantage.
  • →Celebrate Week 1: You've started. That's the hardest step. Add a ✓ to your practice calendar for every day you sit at the piano, even for just 5 minutes.
02
Foundation Phase

Two Hands — Left Hand Intro & Simple Coordination

The left hand awakens. We introduce bass clef, LH position, and the first taste of hands-together playing.

🎯
Section 1
Learning Objectives
+
  • →Read and play C position with the left hand (C–G below Middle C)
  • →Understand bass clef: lines (Good Boys Do Fine Always) and spaces (All Cows Eat Grass)
  • →Play a simple melody with RH while LH holds a single root note
  • →Internalize steady quarter-note pulse at 60–70 BPM with a metronome
🤲
Section 2
Warm-Up & Finger Exercises
+
  • →5-note LH scale (C–G): Slow and deliberate, fingers 5–1, listening for even volume on each note
  • →Mirror exercise: RH and LH play the same 5-note pattern simultaneously (both going up, both going down). Builds symmetrical coordination.
  • →Metronome introduction: Set to 60 BPM. Clap quarter notes. Then play Middle C repeatedly in time. Treat the metronome as a bandmate, not a drill sergeant.
📖
Section 3
Music Theory
+
  • →Bass clef: The F clef — note that the two dots surround the F line. Memorize landmark notes: Bass C, G, and F
  • →Time signature 4/4: 4 beats per measure, quarter note gets 1 beat. Count "1 2 3 4" aloud while playing
  • →Bar lines and measures: The "containers" of music. Draw and label a 4-bar phrase on manuscript paper
👂
Section 4
Ear Training & Improvisation
+
  • →Stepping vs. Skipping: Teacher plays two notes — student identifies if they are "next-door neighbors" (steps) or "jumping" (skips). This is the foundation of interval recognition.
  • →Call & Response: Teacher plays a 2-note phrase in the RH; student answers with 2 notes in the LH. Pure musical conversation — no wrong answers.
🎵
Section 5
Repertoire Suggestions
+
  • →Classical: "Lightly Row" — introduces both hands with a simple LH accompaniment pattern (root notes)
  • →Pop: Simplified "Hallelujah" (Cohen) — just the opening 4-bar phrase, RH only, then add drone LH on C and G
  • →Continue: Polish "Ode to Joy" from Week 1 — now add a simple LH C–C–G–G accompaniment
📱
Section 6
Flowkey App Guided Practice
+
  • →Flowkey Exercise: "Left Hand Training Module" — Follow the app's LH-only exercises in C position. Aim for 3 error-free runs of each exercise.
  • →Flowkey Exercise: "Rhythm Trainer" — Use the app's rhythm tap feature at 60 BPM. Tap along to quarter-note patterns.
  • →Home practice (15–20 min/day): Alternate between LH exercises and reviewing Week 1 song in the app.
🟢 Flowkey App · Week 2 Module · ~20 min/day
💻 Online format: Ask student to position camera to show both hands clearly. Record 30-second clips of practice for asynchronous teacher feedback between sessions.
🏠
Section 7
Offline Practice Activities
+
  • →Rhythmic clapping: Clap while walking: "1 and 2 and 3 and 4" — feel the pulse in your body, not just your fingers
  • →Flash cards: Make 10 note-name flashcards (treble + bass clef). Quiz yourself for 3 minutes before bed
  • →Listen: This week's listening: Debussy's "Clair de Lune" (first 2 minutes). Notice how the left hand creates atmosphere while the right hand sings.
📊
Section 8
Assessment & Progress Tracking
+
  • →LH check: Play C–D–E–F–G with LH, fingers 5–4–3–2–1, even tempo, no peeking at hands after 3 practice sessions
  • →Sight-reading mini-quiz: Teacher writes 4 notes on staff (2 treble, 2 bass). Student names them in 10 seconds.
  • →Flowkey metric: Week 2 module accuracy score (target: 80%+)
✨
Section 9
Motivation & Creative Challenge
+

🎯 Week 2 Creative Challenge

Record yourself playing your Week 1 melody (4-note composition) again. Compare it to last week. Can you hear improvement? Save both recordings. This is your musical time capsule. By Week 8, you'll be amazed by the contrast.

03
Building Phase

Chords Unlocked — Triads, C Major & Playing Together

The moment everything clicks: your first three-note chord. This week transforms single notes into harmony.

🎯
Section 1
Learning Objectives
+
  • →Understand and play C major, F major, and G major triads in root position
  • →Recognize the "skip-a-note" structure of triads (1st, 3rd, 5th scale degrees)
  • →Play I–IV–V chord progression in C major with LH while RH plays melody
  • →Distinguish major (happy) from minor (sad) by sound
🤲
Section 2
Warm-Up & Finger Exercises
+
  • →Chord "drops" (5 min): Lift hand 6 inches above keyboard, drop onto C-E-G chord, let the weight of the arm create the sound. Repeat 10x each hand.
  • →Hanon Exercise No.1 (simplified): Fingers 1-2-3-4-5 and back, C to C, very slowly at 60 BPM. The goal is evenness, not speed.
📖
Section 3
Music Theory
+
  • →Triads: Root, 3rd, 5th. Major = root + 4 semitones + 3 semitones. Minor = root + 3 + 4. Build both on C and compare.
  • →The I-IV-V progression: The most common progression in Western music (blues, pop, folk, rock). In C: C-F-G. Recognizing this opens hundreds of songs.
  • →Intervals — 3rds and 5ths: Visual and physical recognition on the keyboard. A third skips one key; a fifth skips three.
👂
Section 4
Ear Training & Improvisation
+
  • →Major vs Minor listening: Teacher plays C major then C minor. "Happy or sad?" Do 8 rounds with different chords. Start building an internal chord library.
  • →Chord improvisation: LH holds C major chord. RH improvises freely on C-D-E-G-A (C pentatonic). You'll sound musical immediately — this never gets old.
🎵
Section 5
Repertoire Suggestions
+
  • →Pop: "Let It Be" (Beatles) — verse uses C-G-Am-F. Simplified version: LH plays root notes, RH plays melody
  • →Classical: Bach "Minuet in G" — introduce this as a future goal. Just listen this week.
  • →Jazz intro: Simple 12-bar blues in C using I-IV-V. Teacher demonstrates; student plays LH roots on beats 1 and 3.
📱
Section 6
Flowkey App Guided Practice
+
  • →Flowkey Exercise: "Chord Builder" — Use the app's chord recognition tool. Build C, F, and G major on the app keyboard, then transfer to acoustic/digital piano.
  • →Flowkey Exercise: "Song of the Week" — Select a simplified "Let It Be" or equivalent chord-based song in the app's library. Use the slow-down feature to practice at 70% speed.
  • →Daily goal: 3 chord changes (C→F→G→C) without looking at hands by end of week.
🟢 Flowkey App · Week 3 Module · ~20 min/day
💻 Online format: Use Flowkey App's built-in progress dashboard — student shares screen showing chord accuracy scores. Teacher can assign specific in-app exercises remotely.
🏠
Section 7
Offline Practice Activities
+
  • →Chord chart: Draw the three chords (C, F, G) on a paper keyboard diagram. Number the fingers used. Keep it on the music stand.
  • →Song detective: Listen to 3 songs this week and try to hear when the chord changes happen. Tap your foot on beat 1 of each new chord. You'll be surprised how many songs use I-IV-V.
📊
Section 8
Assessment
+
  • →Play C-F-G-C chord progression, LH, 4 times through without stopping (target: 80 BPM)
  • →Identify major vs minor when teacher plays 6 random chords (target: 5/6 correct)
  • →Flowkey App chord accuracy: 85%+ on Week 3 chord exercises
✨
Section 9
Motivation & Creative Challenge
+

🎯 Week 3 Creative Challenge

You now know 3 chords. Hundreds of famous songs use exactly these three chords. Choose one song you love and look up its chord chart online. Try to play the first 4 chords — even if imperfect. This is the moment music becomes personal.

04
Building Phase

Rhythm Deep Dive — Eighth Notes, Syncopation & Groove

Rhythm is the backbone of music. This week we move beyond quarter notes to explore eighth-note patterns and how adult learners can feel the groove, not just count it.

🎯
All Sections
Week 4 Overview
+
  • →Objectives: Read and clap eighth-note patterns; play a melody using quarter + eighth note combinations; understand dotted quarter notes (dotted = 1.5 beats)
  • →Warm-Up: "1-and-2-and" clapping at 70 BPM. Then transfer to keyboard — alternate C and E on every eighth note. Builds subdivision instinct.
  • →Theory: Eighth notes (1/2 beat), dotted rhythms, syncopation intro — placing accents on the "and." Count aloud: "1 AND 2 AND 3 AND 4 AND"
  • →Ear Training: "Rhythm echo" — teacher claps a 2-bar rhythmic pattern, student echoes it exactly. Builds rhythmic memory. Then reverse: student invents, teacher echoes.
  • →Repertoire: "Für Elise" opening (simplified); simplified "Clocks" riff (Coldplay) — both excellent for eighth-note feel in adult context
  • →Flowkey App: "Rhythm Challenge" module — the app's rhythm accuracy trainer with visual waveform feedback. Target: 90% timing accuracy at 75 BPM
  • →Offline: Body percussion — tap thigh on beats 1&3, clap on 2&4 (basic backbeat). Do this while listening to music daily.
  • →Assessment: Perform a 4-bar rhythm from notation at sight; Flowkey accuracy score; RH melody with correct rhythmic values
  • →Challenge: Write your own 2-bar rhythm pattern using only quarter and eighth notes. Teach it to someone else (explaining teaches best).
🟢 Flowkey App · Week 4 Rhythm Module · ~20–25 min/day
💻 Online: Use a shared metronome app (e.g., Metronome Online) so teacher and student click in sync during online sessions.
05
Expanding Phase

Melody & Scales — C Major Scale, Hand Position Shifts

The first full scale unlocks the entire keyboard. Students begin shifting hand position and reading notes beyond the 5-note C position.

🎯
All Sections
Week 5 Overview
+
  • →Objectives: Play C major scale (one octave, RH and LH separately); execute the thumb tuck correctly; read notes through D5 in treble clef
  • →Warm-Up: Slow scale, each note held 2 beats. Then "two octave crawl" — no rush, focus on smooth thumb-under motion. The thumb tuck is the technical milestone of this week.
  • →Theory: W-W-H-W-W-W-H pattern (whole and half steps). Build the C major scale from the pattern and understand why it sounds "right." Introduce the concept of key signature (C major = no sharps or flats).
  • →Ear Training: "Does it resolve?" — play the 7th note (B) and sit on it. Feel the tension. Then play C. Feel the release. This is the basis of all harmonic tension and resolution.
  • →Improvisation: Use the full C major scale to improvise a 4-bar melody. No LH this week — just melody. Focus on musical shape: start low, peak in the middle, resolve at the end.
  • →Repertoire: "Joy to the World" — uses a descending major scale, perfect for week 5; Burgmüller "Arabesque" (introduce slowly); simplified Einaudi "Experience" opening
  • →Flowkey App: "Scale Trainer" module — the app tracks accuracy and smoothness of scale passages. Practice at 80, 90, then 100 BPM. Also use "Note Reading Drill" for notes above the staff.
  • →Offline: Air-piano the thumb-tuck motion while away from the instrument. The muscle memory of passing the thumb under is built partly through mental rehearsal.
  • →Assessment: Play C major scale RH, one octave, at 80 BPM, smooth thumb tuck. Teacher rates: Evenness (1–5), Tone (1–5), Tempo (1–5).
  • →Challenge: Try building a G major scale using the W-W-H pattern. Figure out which note needs a sharp. You've just discovered your second key!
🟢 Flowkey App · Scale & Note Reading Modules · ~25 min/day
06
Expanding Phase

Dynamics & Phrasing — Playing with Emotion

Technique without expression is typing. This week we add the human element: dynamics, legato, staccato, and musical storytelling.

🎯
All Sections
Week 6 Overview
+
  • →Objectives: Understand and apply p, mp, mf, f dynamic markings; play legato phrases with connected, smooth tone; play staccato with a light, detached touch; apply a musical crescendo over 4 bars
  • →Warm-Up: Play the C major scale 4 times: pp (whisper), mp, mf, ff (bold). Then reverse. Develops physical control of key velocity — the only way a piano changes volume.
  • →Theory: Italian dynamic terms (pianissimo to fortissimo), hairpin crescendo/decrescendo, slur vs staccato markings. Introduce fermata (hold) and the concept of phrasing as "musical breathing."
  • →Ear Training: "Tell me the story" — teacher plays the same 4-bar phrase three ways: robotic, with dynamics, with full phrasing. Student describes what changes emotionally. Powerful for adult learners.
  • →Improvisation: Improvise an 8-bar piece that starts soft, builds to a peak (bar 5–6), then fades. The constraint forces intentional musical shaping.
  • →Repertoire: Beethoven "Moonlight Sonata" Mvt. 1 (simplified 4-bar excerpt); Yiruma "River Flows in You" (opening — all about dynamic shaping); revisit "Ode to Joy" and add all dynamics
  • →Flowkey App: Many apps include a velocity/dynamics visualizer — use it to see the height of each note-hit as a bar graph. The visual feedback is extremely powerful. Practice a phrase until the dynamic shape is visible in the graph.
  • →Offline: Sing or hum your current piece while doing dishes or commuting. Singers naturally shape phrases. Hearing your melody vocally informs how you'll play it.
  • →Assessment: Play a 8-bar piece with clear p and f sections. Teacher gives written feedback on 3 things done well and 1 to improve.
  • →Challenge: Take a piece you know well and reinterpret it entirely — play it as if it's a lullaby, then as a march. Same notes, completely different music.
🟢 Flowkey App · Dynamics & Phrasing Module · ~25 min/day
07
Performance Phase

Repertoire Deep Work — Polish & Performance Preparation

Two weeks before the finish line. This week is entirely focused on bringing 2–3 pieces to performance level — musically, technically, and emotionally.

🎯
All Sections
Week 7 Overview
+
  • →Objectives: Perform 2–3 selected pieces from memory or with minimal score reference; apply all learned skills (dynamics, rhythm, phrasing, correct fingering) simultaneously; manage performance anxiety
  • →Warm-Up: "Problem spot" practice — identify the one hardest bar in each piece. Isolate it. Play it 10x slowly. Then integrate it. This is how professionals practice.
  • →Theory: Review — play a chord progression and identify the Roman numeral analysis (I, IV, V). Connect theory to the actual pieces being polished. Theory should illuminate, not burden.
  • →Ear Training: Record yourself playing a piece and listen back critically. Note 2 things you love and 2 things to fix. Self-assessment is a professional skill.
  • →Repertoire for Recital (choose 2–3): "Ode to Joy" (Beethoven, full version with LH chords); "Für Elise" opening; simplified "River Flows in You"; 12-bar blues improvisation (counts as a "piece"); the student's own 4-bar composition from Week 1 (expanded)
  • →Flowkey App: Use the app's "Performance Run-Through" mode (no stopping, real-time score). Simulate performance conditions. Aim for 3 clean run-throughs of each piece per day.
  • →Offline: Mental practice — close your eyes and "play" the piece in your mind, hearing every note. Neuroscience shows this activates the same motor pathways as physical practice.
  • →Performance anxiety tip: Breathe before you begin (2 deep breaths). Focus on the music, not the listener. Remember: adults in the audience want you to succeed.
  • →Assessment: Mock recital — perform all pieces for a friend, family member, or record and self-assess. Debrief: what felt good? What needs one more week?
  • →Challenge: Choose one piece and add your own interpretation — a ritardando at the end, a different dynamic in bar 3, something that makes it undeniably yours.
🟢 Flowkey App · Performance Mode · 3x run-throughs/day per piece
💻 Online: Perform your mock recital via video call with teacher providing live feedback. This is also great performance anxiety practice — performing over video feels surprisingly real.
08
Celebration & Next Steps

Recital Week — Celebrate, Reflect & Look Forward

Eight weeks ago you sat down at a piano for the first time. This week, you perform. And then you plan what comes next.

🎯
All Sections
Week 8 Overview
+
  • →Objectives: Perform a mini-recital of 2–3 polished pieces; demonstrate all skills learned across 8 weeks; articulate personal musical goals for the next 8 weeks
  • →Warm-Up: Your usual routine — now it should feel familiar and grounding. C scale, a chord progression, finger exercises. The ritual calms performance nerves.
  • →Theory Review Showcase: Teacher asks 5 spontaneous theory questions (note names, chord types, time signatures, dynamic markings, scale structure). The student answers verbally — no piano needed. Shows internalized knowledge.
  • →Ear Training Final: Major or minor? Step or skip? High or low? Quick-fire 10-question ear test. Compare to Week 1 baseline.
  • →The Recital Program: Student selects and introduces each piece by name and composer before playing. Presenting your music is part of musicianship.
  • →Flowkey App — Final Report: Review the app's 8-week progress analytics together. Celebrate milestones: accuracy improvements, songs completed, practice streaks. This data tells a story.
  • →Offline Celebration: Listen to a professional recording of one piece you played this week. Notice the difference — and appreciate how far you've come while seeing where the road still leads.
  • →Assessment — Full Portfolio: ① Recital performance (teacher rates: 1–5 each on technique, musicality, rhythm, stage presence) ② Theory quiz (10 questions) ③ Flowkey App 8-week completion report ④ Personal reflection letter: "What did piano teach me about myself?"
  • →Looking Forward — Suggested Next 8 Weeks: G major scale and key signature; Am and Dm chords (minor harmony); 3/4 time signature (waltz feel); Pedal technique (sustain pedal); New repertoire: Clementi Sonatina, jazz standard (simplified), one pop song with full chord accompaniment

Final Word
A Note for the Student
+

🎹 You Did It

Eight weeks ago, the piano was a mystery. Now you can read music, play chords, shape phrases, understand harmony, and perform pieces with intention and feeling. The path ahead is long — and that's wonderful news. Every great pianist you've ever admired once sat exactly where you are now. The instrument is patient. Keep going.

  • →The most important habit: Daily practice, even 10 minutes. Consistency beats marathon sessions every time.
  • →Next milestone to aim for: Learn one piece entirely by ear — no sheet music, no app. Trust what you've built.
  • →Share your music: Play for one person this week who hasn't heard you before. Music is meant to be shared.
